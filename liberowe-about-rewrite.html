The Beginning

India gave her
no words.
So she made a jacket.

Talia had spent years working in fashion. Alexander McQueen, Central Saint Martins, the industry's best rooms. She knew how clothes were made. Then she went to India and something shifted.

It was not the embroideries she expected to find. It was the sherwanis. The strict, structured menswear jackets with Nehru collars and precise pocket detailing. Shapes that felt completely timeless. She became obsessed. She wanted to wear one.

She came back to London, sat at her dining room table, and made the Raja. Not for a collection. For herself. Then she shared it with other women. And the rest, as she says, just flowed.

01 / The Process

She starts with
the fabric.

Not with a sketch. Not with a brief. With the weight of it in her hands, how it drapes, whether it is crisp or fluid. Her background is knitwear. She is tactile before she is visual.

02 / The Method

She drapes on
herself.

In front of a mirror, alone in the studio. It is almost introspective, she says. She is always asking: what do I want to wear? Then she makes it. Then she shares it with other women.

03 / The Standard

Every piece checked
by her hands.

Over 80% of Liberowe is made in London. Everything else is produced by ethical manufacturers in Europe. Fabrics from Italian, French and British mills, as well as UK deadstock. Not as a policy. As basic values.

The Liberowe Woman

She questions things.
That is the whole point.

Talia grew up with four brothers. She has always questioned her sense of femininity and her relationship to masculinity, and how the two can meet. Becoming a mother made that question sharper.

The Liberowe woman carries that same dialogue. She is not one thing. She is attracted to boldness and delicacy at once, to freedom and structure in the same garment. She understands that a well-made jacket does not just dress her. It gives her poise. Presence. A sense of who she is, expressed without explanation.

"Once the clothes leave me," Talia says, "they belong to the woman's wardrobe. How she styles and owns them becomes part of the story."

On tailoring
Talia Loubaton
Founder and Creative Director, Liberowe
"Tailoring gives confidence. When you wear heels, you feel taller. When you wear a jacket, you feel stronger. That is central to my design process. I always start with tailoring to define the shape and silhouette. It is like creating a picture."
The Commitment

Clean and ethical
was never a choice.
It was the only way.

When Talia developed Liberowe, she says, there was no other way to do it than cleanly. Not because sustainability was a trend. Because making clothes irresponsibly felt like a contradiction she could not live with.

Fabrics are sourced from Italian and French mills, as well as UK deadstock. Everything is produced in small quantities, by people Talia knows. Private orders and bespoke pieces are available for those who want something made specifically for them. That conversation started in a friend's living room in 2022. It is still the same conversation.

Copywriter's Notes
What changed and why

The Opening Statement

The live About page opens with: "Liberowe is a brand of luxury tailoring made in London. A clashing dynamic between two realities: Indian menswear and Paris in the seventies." That is accurate but it reads like a press release summary, not a founder's voice. The rewrite opens with Talia's own distinction about her creative process: "I make. I do not design." She said this in an interview with Marie Claire in April 2026. It is the most honest and specific thing she has ever said about what Liberowe is. It belongs at the top of the page, not buried in a magazine feature.

The India Origin Story

The live About page mentions India in passing: "inspired by both Indian menswear and Paris in the seventies." But Talia's account of her India trip, in her own words, is one of the most powerful founding stories in London fashion right now. "The noise, the chaos, the beauty. It silenced me. I came back with the urge to create. I had no words. Just emotions I wanted to translate into clothes." That sentence is extraordinary. It is not on the website anywhere. The rewrite gives it the space it has earned.

The Process Section

The live About page describes Liberowe's aesthetic and influences in conceptual terms. It does not describe how Talia actually works. Yet her process is what makes Liberowe genuinely distinct from every other London tailoring brand. She drapes on herself in front of a mirror. She starts with the fabric in her hands, not with a sketch. It is introspective before it is commercial. This rewrite puts that process on the page because it is the most compelling thing about how these clothes are made.

The Newsletter Capture

The current newsletter footer says: "Receive exclusive access to the latest." That is already stronger than most brands at this stage. But it does not tell you what the access is to, or why it is worth having. The rewrite positions it as a direct line to Talia and the stories behind each collection. "Written when there is something worth sharing. For the women who want to know more than just what to wear." That is the promise Liberowe already makes through its work. The newsletter copy should say so explicitly.

Before and After: The Opening
Live copy

"Liberowe is a brand of luxury tailoring made in London. A clashing dynamic between two realities: Indian menswear and Paris in the seventies. A fresh balance between Orient and Occident, femininity and masculinity, strength and delicacy."

Accurate. But written at arm's length. Tells you what to think about the brand before giving you anything to feel. Reads like a brand brief, not a founder.
Rewrite

"I make. I do not design. Talia Loubaton started Liberowe at her dining room table in March 2022 with a rail of clothes and a notebook. She had no business plan. She had a jacket, an idea, and hands that already knew what to do. Everything else followed."

Opens with Talia's own words from Marie Claire, April 2026. Grounds the brand in a specific, human moment rather than a conceptual positioning statement. The visitor feels Talia before she is asked to understand her.
